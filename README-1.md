## 📌 Project Overview

**NeuroWell Analytics** — a global leader in workplace well-being and productivity solutions — engaged me as **Lead Data Scientist / ML Engineer** to design and deploy a machine learning solution that predicts employee burnout rates from historical HR data.

In today's fast-paced work environment, employee burnout silently erodes productivity, morale, mental health, and long-term organisational resilience — often before leadership has any visibility into the problem. The resulting model equips HR teams and senior leadership to **proactively intervene** before burnout escalates into attrition, absenteeism, or a full mental health crisis.

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Business Problem** | Predict the psychological saturation level (burn rate) of each employee so HR can prioritise welfare interventions |
| **Target Variable** | `burn_rate` — continuous score from `0.0` (no burnout) to `1.0` (complete burnout) |
| **Project Type** | Supervised Machine Learning — Regression |
| **Training Dataset** | 22,750 employee records × 9 features |
| **Test Dataset** | 12,250 employee records × 8 features |
| **Best Model** | Gradient Boosting Regressor |
| **Model File** | `best_burnout_prediction_model.joblib` |
| **Deployment** | Streamlit Cloud → [adewale-burnout-prediction.streamlit.app](https://adewale-burnout-prediction.streamlit.app) |

## 📊 Model Performance

Three regression models were trained, evaluated, and compared on the same 80/20 validation split before selecting the best:

| Metric | Linear Regression | Random Forest | ✅ Gradient Boosting |
|--------|:-----------------:|:-------------:|:--------------------:|
| **R² Score** | 0.8304 | 0.8347 | **0.855** |
| **RMSE** | 0.0781 | 0.0771 | **0.072** |
| **MAE** | 0.0569 | 0.0568 | **0.053** |

> **Selection rationale:** The Gradient Boosting Regressor outperforms both competitors on all three metrics. Its sequential residual-correction mechanism captures non-linear feature interactions that Linear Regression misses, while its regularised boosting outperforms the Random Forest's averaging approach. The model explains **85.5% of burn rate variance** and predicts with a mean absolute error of just **0.053 points** on a 0–1 scale — approximately 5.3 burn rate units of average error.

### Risk Tier Distribution (Test Set — 12,250 Employees)

| Risk Tier | Burn Rate Range | Employees | % of Test Set |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|---------------|
| 🟢 Low Risk | ≤ 0.33 | 1,768 | 14.4% |
| 🟡 Moderate Risk | 0.34 – 0.55 | 8,193 | 66.9% |
| 🔴 High Risk | 0.56 – 0.75 | 2,289 | 18.7% |
| 🚨 Critical Risk | > 0.75 | 0 | 0.0% |

> Nearly **1 in 5 employees** (18.7%) is predicted in the High Risk tier — a substantial and immediately actionable segment for NeuroWell Analytics clients.

## 🔑 Key Findings

| # | Finding | Statistical Evidence |
|---|---------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Mental fatigue is the dominant burnout driver** | Pearson r = **+0.878** with `burn_rate`; **85.9%** Gradient Boosting feature importance |
| 2 | **Workload is the second-largest driver** | `resource_allocation` r = **+0.810**; Q4 employees carry **2.7× higher workload** than Q1 (mean: 6.57 vs 2.40) |
| 3 | **WFH access is the most actionable policy lever** | No-WFH mean burn rate = **0.518** vs WFH mean = **0.396** — a **12.2pp gap** |
| 4 | **Senior employees are the highest-risk subgroup** | `designation` r = **+0.719**; Q4 average designation = **3.25** vs Q1 = **1.13** |
| 5 | **Company type is statistically irrelevant** | Mean burn rate difference between Service and Product = **0.18pp** only |
| 6 | **Target is near-normally distributed** | Mean = **0.452**, Median = **0.450**, Skewness = **+0.048** — no transformation required |

## 🧩 Feature Engineering Details

| Feature | Source | Derivation | Justification |
|---------|--------|------------|---------------|
| `tenure_months` | `date_of_joining` | Reference date (2009-01-01) minus join date, converted to integer months | Captures organisational longevity as a potential burnout risk factor |
| `gender_Male` | `gender` | One-Hot Encoded, Female as reference class | Removes string dependency; eliminates dummy variable trap |
| `company_type_Service` | `company_type` | One-Hot Encoded, Product as reference class | Enables model to use company type as a binary signal |
| `wfh_setup_available_Yes` | `wfh_setup_available` | One-Hot Encoded, No as reference class | WFH access is the single most actionable policy variable |
| `employee_id` | Dropped | Non-predictive anonymised identifier | Zero signal; would introduce noise |
| `date_of_joining` | Dropped | Replaced by `tenure_months` | Raw date is not usable by scikit-learn estimators |

**Final feature set (7 predictors):**
`designation` · `resource_allocation` · `mental_fatigue_score` · `tenure_months` · `gender_Male` · `company_type_Service` · `wfh_setup_available_Yes`

## 💡 Business Recommendations (Summary)

| Priority | Recommendation | Evidence |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| 🔴 HIGH | **Quarterly mental fatigue pulse surveys** — trigger welfare check-in for scores > 7.5/10 | Feature importance 85.9%, r = +0.878 |
| 🔴 HIGH | **Workload caps at 8/10** for sustained periods > 4 weeks | Q4 employees average 6.57 resource allocation vs Q1's 2.40 |
| 🔴 HIGH | **Mandate minimum 2 WFH days/week** for eligible roles | 12.2pp burn rate gap between No-WFH and WFH groups |
| 🟡 MEDIUM | **Senior Wellbeing Programme** targeting Designation 4–5 | Senior + No-WFH = highest-risk intersectional subgroup |
| 🟡 MEDIUM | **Monthly HR scoring engine** using the deployed model | Enables continuous monitoring vs quarterly snapshots |
| 🟢 LOW | **Annual model retraining pipeline** triggered by PSI monitoring | Prevents model drift as workforce dynamics evolve |
